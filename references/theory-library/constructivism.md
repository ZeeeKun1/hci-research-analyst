# Constructivist Learning Theory

## Overview

Constructivism, rooted in Piaget's cognitive constructivism and Vygotsky's social constructivism, holds that learners actively construct knowledge through experience and social interaction, rather than passively receiving information. Fundamental to educational technology and learning systems design.

## Core Constructs

### Piaget — Cognitive Constructivism
- **Assimilation**: Incorporating new experience into existing cognitive structures (schemas).
- **Accommodation**: Modifying existing schemas when new experience doesn't fit.
- **Equilibration**: The drive to balance assimilation and accommodation. Cognitive growth occurs through disequilibrium → accommodation → new equilibrium.
- **Stages**: Sensorimotor → Preoperational → Concrete operational → Formal operational (developmental progression).

### Vygotsky — Social Constructivism
- **Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD)**: The gap between what a learner can do independently and what they can do with guidance.
- **Scaffolding**: Temporary support structures that help learners bridge the ZPD. Gradually removed as competence increases.
- **Mediation**: Cognitive tools (language, symbols, technology) mediate learning.
- **Internalization**: External social processes become internal cognitive processes.

## Key Predictions

1. Active engagement produces deeper learning than passive reception.
2. Prior knowledge strongly influences what and how much is learned.
3. Social interaction accelerates learning when it creates cognitive conflict.
4. Scaffolding is most effective when matched to the learner's ZPD.
5. Metacognitive awareness (thinking about thinking) enhances learning outcomes.
6. Concrete experiences followed by abstraction produces robust understanding.
7. Collaborative learning can be more effective than individual learning (under right conditions).

## HCI Applications

| Context | Application |
|---------|-------------|
| Educational technology | Design active, experiential learning environments |
| Onboarding systems | Scaffold complex tasks; match difficulty to user level |
| Interactive tutorials | Support exploration with guidance (guided discovery) |
| Simulation/VR training | Concrete experience before abstract instruction |
| Adaptive learning | Detect ZPD and adjust scaffolding dynamically |
| Museums/exhibits | Design for hands-on, constructivist engagement |

## When Findings Map to Constructivism

- Users learn better through doing than reading → Active construction of knowledge
- Users struggle at first then "click" → Accommodation after disequilibrium
- Users benefit from guidance but resist over-instruction → Scaffolding in ZPD
- Users create their own understanding that differs from intended → Individual knowledge construction
- Collaborative learners outperform individuals → Social mediation effect
- Users transfer learning to new contexts → Schema formation and application
