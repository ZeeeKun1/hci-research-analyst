# Discourse Analysis / Conversation Analysis

## Overview

Discourse Analysis (DA) and Conversation Analysis (CA) examine how language is used in social interaction. DA focuses on broader discourse practices and power relations; CA focuses on the micro-organization of talk (turn-taking, repair, sequence organization). In HCI, both are valuable for analyzing user interviews, think-aloud protocols, and system-mediated conversations.

## When to Use

- Analyzing how users talk about technology (interviews, focus groups)
- Analyzing how people interact through technology (chat, voice interfaces, social media)
- Analyzing think-aloud protocols during usability studies
- Studying power dynamics in technology-mediated communication
- Understanding how interaction order is negotiated in multi-user systems

## Core Concepts — Conversation Analysis

1. **Turn-Taking System**: How speakers allocate turns (transition relevance places, overlap, gap).
2. **Sequence Organization**: Adjacency pairs (question-answer, request-grant), preference structure.
3. **Repair**: How participants fix problems in understanding (self-repair vs other-repair).
4. **Opening/Closing**: How interactions begin and end.
5. **Membership Categorization**: How speakers categorize themselves and others.

## Core Concepts — Discourse Analysis

1. **Discursive Construction**: How realities are constructed through language.
2. **Subject Positions**: How speakers position themselves and others (e.g., "I'm not technical").
3. **Intertextuality**: How texts reference other texts and discourses.
4. **Power & Ideology**: How language reproduces or challenges power relations.
5. **Rhetorical Strategies**: How speakers persuade, justify, or resist.

## Procedure

### 1. Transcription
- For CA: Use Jefferson transcription conventions (detailed, captures timing, overlap, intonation).
- For DA: Standard orthographic transcription may suffice, with notes on emphasis and pauses.
- Include contextual notes (what's happening on screen, nonverbal behavior).

### 2. Identify Analytic Focus
Based on research question, select one or more:
- Turn-taking patterns (e.g., how users and voice assistants negotiate)
- Repair sequences (e.g., how users recover from system errors)
- Topic management (e.g., how participants structure their narratives)
- Subject positioning (e.g., how users construct their identity as "non-experts")
- Persuasion/resistance strategies

### 3. Analytic Process
- Work from specific instances to general patterns.
- Do not prematurely abstract — show the detailed mechanism.
- Pay attention to what is NOT said (silences, hesitations, topic avoidance).
- Compare across participants and conditions.

### 4. Build the Argument
- Select compelling examples that illustrate the pattern.
- Show the mechanism: HOW does this interaction work?
- Connect to broader theoretical concerns.
- Acknowledge deviant cases.

## HCI-Specific Applications

| Context | What to Analyze |
|---------|----------------|
| Voice interfaces (VUI) | Turn-taking between user and system, repair, misalignment |
| Think-aloud studies | How users narrate their reasoning; what they choose to verbalize |
| Focus groups | How participants build on/challenge each other's views |
| Online forums | How community norms are negotiated through language |
| Interview studies | How users construct their experience narrative |

## Key Considerations

- Think-aloud data is inherently discursive — what users say is a performance, not a direct window into cognition. Interpret accordingly.
- In multilingual HCI contexts, code-switching and language choice itself can be analytically rich.
- Technology-mediated conversations (chatbots, VUI) create novel interactional structures that CA is well-suited to analyze.
