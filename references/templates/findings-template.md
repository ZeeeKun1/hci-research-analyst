# Findings Section Template

Template for writing CHI-style findings sections. Adapt structure and headings to your paper's specific needs.

---

## [Section Title: e.g., "Results" or "Findings"]

[Brief introductory paragraph: 1-2 sentences overviewing what was found and how the analysis was conducted. Reference the analysis method.]

> We analyzed [data type] from [N] participants using [method]. [One sentence summary of main finding arc.]

---

### [T1: Theme Name]

[2-3 sentences introducing the theme. What is this about? Why does it matter? Connect to the broader research question.]

> "The first theme captures [essence of theme]. Participants [did/felt/experienced X], which [implication]."

**[T1.1: Sub-theme (if applicable)]**

[1-2 sentences explaining the sub-theme. Be specific about the pattern.]

[Follow with illustrative quote. Use ellipsis (...) for cuts within quotes, brackets [text] for clarifying insertions. Include participant ID and brief context if needed.]

> "[Illustrative quote from participant]" — P[XX]

[Add 1-2 sentences of analytic interpretation. What does this mean? Why did participants say/do this? Connect to theory if applicable.]

> "This pattern suggests [interpretation]. As [Author, Year] describes [theory], [explanation of how theory applies]."

[If quantitative data supports this theme:]
> Consistent with these qualitative accounts, [quantitative finding], t(df) = X.XX, p = .XXX, d = X.XX.

---

### [T2: Theme Name]

[Same structure as above]

[Continue for all themes]

---

### [T3: Theme Name — Unexpected/Contradictory Finding (if applicable)]

[If findings contradict theory or common assumptions, highlight this explicitly.]

> "Interestingly, [unexpected finding]. This runs counter to [common assumption / theory prediction], suggesting [interpretation]. Rather than [expected], participants [did something different]."

[This is often the most valuable finding for reviewers.]

---

### [Integration Section — for mixed-methods studies]

[If using mixed methods, include a dedicated integration section or table.]

> Table X: Joint Display of Quantitative and Qualitative Findings
>
> | Theme | Quantitative Evidence | Qualitative Evidence | Integration |
> |-------|----------------------|---------------------|-------------|
> | [T1] | [Finding + stats] | [Finding + quote] | [Synthesis] |
> | [T2] | [Finding + stats] | [Finding + quote] | [Synthesis] |

[2-3 sentences discussing where methods converged and where they diverged.]

---

### [Summary / Discussion Bridge]

[Brief paragraph (3-4 sentences) summarizing the key takeaways across all themes. This should set up the Discussion section.]

> "In summary, our analysis reveals [main insight 1], [main insight 2], and [main insight 3]. These findings [advance understanding of / challenge assumptions about / provide new perspective on] [research topic]. We discuss the implications for [design/theory/future work] in the Discussion."

---

## Writing Guidelines

1. **Lead with the theme, not the method**: The findings section tells the story, not the process. Method details go in the Methods section.
2. **Every theme needs**: description + evidence (quotes/data) + interpretation.
3. **Quote placement**: Weave quotes into the narrative. Don't just dump them. Introduce each quote's significance.
4. **Theory integration**: When using theory, be specific about the mechanism. Not "this relates to affordance theory" but "the mismatch between perceived and actual affordance [Norman, 1988] caused..."
5. **Confidence markers**: Use "suggests," "indicates," "points to" rather than "proves" or "shows." Academic writing is appropriately tentative.
6. **Participant diversity**: If different groups responded differently, note it. Don't erase variation in pursuit of a clean narrative.
7. **Length**: Typically 2-4 pages (800-2000 words) for a CHI paper's findings section, depending on complexity and contribution type.
